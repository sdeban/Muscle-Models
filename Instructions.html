Muscle Explorer

Basic Instructions

The model simulates these elements in series: muscle-damper-spring-lever-projectile. The muscle begins activation at time zero, builds force and shortens, stretching the spring. The spring is attached by a massless lever to a projectile. You can specify many properties such as the Vmax and P0 of the muscle, the mass of the projectile, the length of the lever (i.e., the mechanical advantage or gear ratio), and the conditions under which the lever is allowed to move.

Duration: Duration of simulation in seconds.

Steps: Number of time steps in integration. Increase if traces are jittery. Latch Time: Start changing the mechanical advantage or gearing between the spring and the projectile at this time--the 'latch'--unless other conditions are met first. Length threshold: Start leverage change when this muscle length is reached, as fraction of muscle L0. Force threshold: Start leverage change at this muscle force, as fraction of muscle P0. Latch duration: Duration of the change in leverage in seconds. Initial gear ratio: Starting gear ratio of leverage change, the reciprocal of mechanical advantage. Final gear ratio. Ending gear ratio. A value of 1 would be no mechanical advantage. Muscle L0: Muscle length in meters at which isometric force peaks. Not the same as resting or initial length, below. Muscle P0: Maximum isometric muscle force in newtons. Muscle Vmax (m/s): Maximum velocity of unloaded muscle shortening in meters per second. Muscle F-V Curvature: Curvature of force-velocity relation. Higher is more curved. Value of 3 is about right for frog limb muscle with a power ratio of 1.1. Muscle init. length: Muscle length at start of trial, as fraction of muscle L0. Excitation: Duration of muscle excitation in seconds. Projectile mass: Mass of the projectile in kg. Spring constant: Stiffness of spring between muscle and projectile, in newtons per meter. Spring slack length: Length in meters at which spring exerts no force. Damping coef: Viscous damping of the inertial muscle mass. Higher values reduce vibrations that can occur with small muscle mass and stiff spring. Inertial mass: Muscle mass that is accelerated when the muscle shortens, as a fraction of muscle mass. Higher values reduce vibration. If the simulation gets sluggish, as it may on some browsers, reload the page.
